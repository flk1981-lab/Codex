## Scope

- Report window: high-value tuberculosis clinical research and official policy signals surfaced in the previous 30 days, with exact publication or release dates shown where available.
- Purpose: give a first formal mentor-style learning report that improves clinical research judgement, not just awareness of headlines.

## 1. Today's 3 to 5 Most Worthwhile Items

### 1. Long-term mortality after TB diagnosis and treatment

- FACT: A Nature Medicine study published online on 2026-03-19 reported that mortality remained elevated after treatment for drug-susceptible pulmonary TB in South Africa, with risk highest early but still persisting long term.
- FACT: This was a population-based matched cohort using linked routine data rather than a randomized trial.
- INFERENCE: The paper pushes us to stop treating "treatment success" as the last clinically meaningful endpoint.
- HYPOTHESIS: Future TB-DM and post-TB protocols may need explicit post-treatment survival and cardiopulmonary follow-up instead of ending attention at microbiological cure.
- Source: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-026-04294-w

### 2. Inpatient same-day 1HP in advanced HIV plus cryptococcal meningitis

- FACT: A Lancet HIV strategy trial in Uganda showed that inpatient initiation of 1HP preventive therapy was non-inferior to deferred outpatient initiation in adults with advanced HIV disease and cryptococcal meningitis, with better completion.
- FACT: The published design was an open-label, randomized, non-inferiority trial with a 15% margin.
- INFERENCE: This is a strong example of a delivery-timing trial answering a real implementation bottleneck without inventing a new drug regimen.
- HYPOTHESIS: Similar "when to start" strategy trials may be highly valuable in TB-DM and other high-risk TB care pathways.
- Source: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41662846/

### 3. Pulmonary rehabilitation after pulmonary TB treatment

- FACT: A Chest randomized trial in Uganda found that pulmonary rehabilitation improved exercise capacity at 6 weeks in adults treated for pulmonary TB.
- FACT: The trial used a patient-centered functional endpoint rather than a microbiological one.
- INFERENCE: Post-TB care is becoming a legitimate intervention field, not merely long-term observation.
- HYPOTHESIS: TB programs that only track recurrence may miss a large share of clinically meaningful post-treatment morbidity.
- Source: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41796938/

### 4. WHO started collecting evidence for the next TB screening policy update

- FACT: On 2026-03-17, WHO issued a public call for data to inform updates on tools for screening for TB disease.
- FACT: The call explicitly mentioned tools such as symptom screening, chest X-ray with or without CAD, C-reactive protein in people living with HIV, and low-complexity automated NAATs, while inviting evidence on newer tools and pediatric use cases.
- INFERENCE: Screening guidance may move before treatment guidance does, so diagnostic and implementation studies should align now with the questions WHO is preparing to revisit.
- HYPOTHESIS: Studies that are not designed around forthcoming WHO screening PICO questions may be less influential even if technically well done.
- Source: https://www.who.int/news-room/articles-detail/public-call-for-data-to-inform-who-policy-updates-on-tools-for-screening-for-tb-disease

### Also of note

- FACT: A Nature Medicine paper published online on 2026-03-10 reported a simplified concentrated-urine workflow that improved active TB detection in retrospective validation cohorts.
- INFERENCE: The clinically relevant message is not only "better sensitivity", but "better sensitivity plus workflow simplification".
- HYPOTHESIS: Prospective near-clinic implementation studies are still needed before this can influence routine triage pathways.
- Source: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-025-04164-x

## 2. Mentor-Style Deep Dive

### Focus paper: long-term mortality after TB diagnosis and treatment

- FACT: The core question was not whether treatment cures TB microbiologically, but whether treated TB patients return to baseline mortality risk.
- FACT: Because the design was observational and population-based, its strength lies in real-world scale and downstream relevance, not causal purity.
- FACT: The main clinical signal was persistent excess mortality after treatment completion.

### Why this paper matters

- INFERENCE: Many TB studies still act as if the clinically meaningful story ends at treatment completion or relapse status. This paper argues that the outcome horizon is too short.
- INFERENCE: For someone designing TB-DM research, this is a reminder that metabolic comorbidity may amplify post-TB risk even if short-term microbiology looks good.

### Methods teaching points

- FACT: Matched cohort designs can answer questions that randomized trials cannot realistically or ethically answer at long follow-up scale.
- INFERENCE: The key threat is residual confounding, so these papers should change how we frame questions, but not be overread as definitive causal proof.
- HYPOTHESIS: A high-value next step would be a post-TB prospective cohort with predefined cardiopulmonary, functional, and mortality outcomes rather than only registry linkage.

## 3. New Methods Lessons From This Run

- FACT: Delivery-strategy trials can be just as decision-relevant as regimen-comparison trials when the practical barrier is uptake or timing.
- FACT: Post-TB intervention trials can legitimately use functional recovery endpoints if the clinical question is recovery rather than sterilization.
- FACT: Official policy-scoping calls from WHO are not yet guideline changes, but they are early signals about which study designs will become more policy-relevant soon.
- INFERENCE: Our current TB learning should not be trapped inside "regimen efficacy" thinking; screening, implementation timing, and post-TB outcomes are now equally important.
- HYPOTHESIS: The most fundable future TB studies may increasingly be hybrid designs that connect microbiology, function, and implementation.

## 4. What Matters Most For Our Study Design Thinking

- FACT: A protocol can be internally polished and still be strategically weak if it ignores where TB evidence and policy are moving.
- FACT: This run reinforces three design priorities: longer post-treatment thinking, implementation-timing questions, and patient-centered recovery outcomes.
- INFERENCE: For TDSC-like work, the next leap in sophistication is not more protocol text but better linkage to external evidence on who benefits, who remains at risk, and what outcomes matter after treatment ends.
- HYPOTHESIS: If we build future TB-DM studies with only microbiological endpoints and no post-treatment functional or survival lens, they may look increasingly incomplete.

## 5. The One Thing You Should Study Next This Week

- Recommendation: make a one-page gap map titled "What outcomes matter after TB treatment ends?"
- FACT: This run produced direct support for three domains: mortality, functional recovery, and implementation timing.
- INFERENCE: That gap map will sharpen which of our future questions are genuinely clinically important versus only operationally convenient.
- HYPOTHESIS: Once that gap map is built, our next study ideas will likely become more mature and less protocol-centered.
