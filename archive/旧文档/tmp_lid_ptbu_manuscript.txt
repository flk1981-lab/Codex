Breathomics for non-sputum triage of bacteriologically unconfirmed pulmonary tuberculosis: a prospective diagnostic-accuracy study

Summary
Background: Bacteriologically unconfirmed pulmonary tuberculosis (PTB-U) remains a common diagnostic problem, particularly when sputum is unavailable, microbiological tests are negative despite compatible clinical findings, or treatment decisions cannot wait for bacteriological confirmation. We aimed to determine whether a breathomics classifier developed in bacteriologically confirmed pulmonary tuberculosis (PTB+) could be locked prospectively and applied without retraining or recalibration in PTB-U as a hospital-based non-sputum triage approach.
Methods: In this prospective diagnostic-accuracy study at Shenzhen Third People's Hospital, Shenzhen, China, consecutive adults undergoing evaluation for suspected pulmonary tuberculosis were enrolled. Exhaled breath was analysed with high-pressure photon ionisation time-of-flight mass spectrometry. A deep-learning classifier was developed in stage 1 using PTB+ and non-TB controls from the stage-1/2 analytic cohort; a single decision threshold was prespecified in an internal validation set by maximising sensitivity while maintaining specificity of at least 80%. The locked classifier was then applied unchanged in three prespecified downstream evaluations: a pooled primary intended-use analysis in PTB-U, a strictly independent stage-2 benchmark analysis, and a temporal stage-3 cohort enrolled after model locking.
Findings: Among 703 screened individuals, 697 were enrolled and 690 had analysable breath spectra after quality control. The stage-1/2 analytic cohort comprised 652 participants: 316 PTB+, 166 PTB-U, and 170 non-TB. Stage 1 included 389 participants in the training set and 80 in the internal validation set, with 17 additional non-TB controls held out a priori for stage 2. The prespecified stage-3 temporal cohort comprised 87 participants (24 PTB+, eight PTB-U, and 55 non-TB). In stage-1 internal validation, the locked operating point yielded an area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC) of 0.876, sensitivity of 92.1%, and specificity of 88.2%. In the pooled primary intended-use analysis (174 PTB-U vs 72 independent non-TB controls), the classifier achieved an AUC of [AUC_primary] (95% CI [CI_primary_AUC]), sensitivity of [Sensitivity_primary]% (95% CI [CI_primary_sensitivity]), and specificity of [Specificity_primary]% (95% CI [CI_primary_specificity]). In the strictly independent stage-2 benchmark analysis (166 PTB-U vs 17 held-out non-TB controls), the classifier achieved an AUC of 0.841 (95% CI 0.765-0.915), sensitivity of 86.1% (95% CI 80.1-90.6), and specificity of 82.3% (95% CI 58.9-93.8). In stage 3, performance was directionally consistent, with no clear evidence of marked deterioration in the temporally separated cohort.
Interpretation: A breathomics classifier trained in PTB+ could be locked prospectively and applied without retraining or recalibration to PTB-U. These findings suggest that breathomics could provide a feasible hospital-based non-sputum triage approach to prioritise further microbiological work-up and closer clinical evaluation in a tertiary-care setting. The present data do not support replacement of microbiological confirmation and should be viewed as implementation-informing rather than definitive implementation evidence.

Research in context
Evidence before this study: Before designing this study, we searched PubMed, Embase, and Web of Science from database inception to [insert search date], using terms related to tuberculosis, exhaled breath, breathomics, volatile organic compounds, mass spectrometry, artificial intelligence, machine learning, diagnosis, triage, and screening. Published studies suggested that breath-based signatures can distinguish tuberculosis from non-tuberculosis controls under experimental conditions. However, most reports were small, single-centre, or case-control in design; many relied mainly on bacteriologically confirmed disease; and few evaluated a locked model prospectively. We found little evidence that breathomics had been tested as a prespecified non-sputum triage tool in the clinically relevant population of bacteriologically unconfirmed pulmonary tuberculosis.
Added value of this study: This prospective diagnostic-accuracy study evaluated a locked breathomics classifier in PTB-U without retraining or recalibration. The study was designed around the intended-use population rather than around repeated discrimination in PTB+. Its validation framework combined three complementary elements: a pooled primary intended-use analysis to improve precision of specificity estimation, a strictly independent stage-2 benchmark using held-out controls, and a prespecified temporal cohort enrolled after model locking.
Implications of all the available evidence: Taken together with previous work, these findings suggest that breathomics might complement, rather than replace, established tuberculosis diagnostics. In hospital practice, it may help prioritise patients with suspected PTB-U for further microbiological work-up and closer clinical evaluation. Broader implementation will require multicentre external validation, cross-site calibration work, and prospective pathway studies to determine clinical utility, workflow effects, and cost-effectiveness.
Introduction
Tuberculosis remains a leading infectious cause of death worldwide, and pulmonary tuberculosis continues to account for most transmission.1 Despite the increasing availability of rapid molecular tests, diagnosis still depends heavily on obtaining adequate respiratory specimens.2 In routine care, this dependence creates a persistent clinical gap: some patients cannot expectorate sputum, some have repeated negative microbiological tests despite compatible imaging and symptoms, and some require treatment decisions before bacteriological confirmation can be secured.3,4
This gap is especially important for bacteriologically unconfirmed pulmonary tuberculosis (PTB-U). Patients with PTB-U are often managed on the basis of integrated clinical judgement rather than microbiological proof alone, and the resulting uncertainty can delay diagnosis, prolong inappropriate empirical treatment, or trigger repeated investigations that still fail to provide definitive confirmation.5 A non-sputum triage tool would be most relevant in this population, where the aim is not to replace microbiological confirmation but to support earlier risk stratification and prioritisation for further work-up.6
Breathomics is a plausible candidate for this role. Exhaled breath sampling is rapid, non-invasive, and potentially repeatable at the point of care.7 Complex mixtures of volatile and semi-volatile compounds in breath may reflect host-pathogen interactions, inflammatory responses, and other disease-related physiological changes.8,9 However, the current evidence base remains limited. Published breath-based tuberculosis studies have often been small, case-control in structure, or focused mainly on discrimination in bacteriologically confirmed disease. Locked prospective evaluation in the clinically relevant PTB-U population has rarely been reported.10-13
We therefore did a prospective diagnostic-accuracy study to determine whether a breathomics classifier developed in bacteriologically confirmed pulmonary tuberculosis could be locked prospectively and applied without retraining or recalibration to PTB-U. We also prespecified a strictly independent benchmark analysis and a temporally separated validation cohort to test whether any apparent signal persisted beyond the development partition and remained directionally consistent after model locking.
Methods
Study design and participants
We did a prospective, single-centre diagnostic-accuracy study at Shenzhen Third People's Hospital, a tertiary tuberculosis referral centre in Shenzhen, China. Consecutive adults undergoing evaluation for possible pulmonary tuberculosis were screened for participation between Jan 1, 2021, and Aug 31, 2023. The study was structured in three prespecified stages. Stage 1 comprised model development and threshold locking within the stage-1/2 analytic cohort. Stage 2 provided a strictly independent benchmark evaluation in the intended-use population of PTB-U against non-TB controls held out a priori from development. Stage 3 was a temporal cohort enrolled after model locking to assess robustness over time.
Eligible participants were adults aged 18 years or older undergoing diagnostic work-up for suspected pulmonary tuberculosis and able to provide an exhaled breath sample. We excluded individuals with no usable breath sample, breath spectra failing prespecified quality-control procedures, isolated extrapulmonary tuberculosis without pulmonary involvement, withdrawal of consent, or insufficient clinical information for final reference-standard classification.
PTB-U was treated as the intended-use population because this is the group in whom a non-sputum triage tool would be most clinically relevant. The study was approved by the institutional ethics committee of Shenzhen Third People's Hospital, and all participants provided written informed consent before any study-specific procedures.
Procedures
After enrolment and before knowledge of final reference-standard classification, participants provided exhaled breath samples according to a standard operating procedure intended to reduce environmental contamination and inter-operator variability. Samples were collected under routine hospital conditions rather than in an artificial laboratory-only setting.
Breath samples were analysed using high-pressure photon ionisation time-of-flight mass spectrometry. Raw spectra underwent a prespecified preprocessing workflow including noise handling, peak alignment, signal normalisation, and feature standardisation before model input. This workflow was fixed during model development and then applied unchanged after model locking.
The breathomics classifier was a deep-learning model developed in stage 1 to distinguish PTB+ from non-TB controls, using a 1D convolutional neural network with spatial attention.14 Stage-1 participants were randomly partitioned into a training set and an internal validation set. Model fitting used the training set only and optimised a composite objective combining focal loss, prototypical loss, contrastive loss, and a Deep SVDD-based regularisation term to improve class discrimination, representation compactness, and robustness of the latent space.15-18 The internal validation set was used to obtain an unbiased estimate of discrimination and to prespecify a single operating threshold before any PTB-U or temporal evaluation. For exploratory representation analyses, embeddings from the locked network were projected into two dimensions using t-distributed stochastic neighbour embedding (t-SNE) with fixed settings and a fixed random seed; these visualisations were hypothesis-generating only, were not used for model fitting or threshold selection, and attention-based rankings were interpreted as model-centric saliency rather than causal attribution.19,20
In stage 2 and stage 3, the locked system was applied without retraining, recalibration, or threshold adjustment. Breathomics results were not used in clinical decision-making, microbiological testing strategy, or final reference-standard assignment.
Reference standard and outcome definitions
Final disease status was determined using a composite reference standard based on microbiological, radiological, clinical, and follow-up information available from routine care. Reference assignment was made independently of the breathomics output.5
Participants were classified into three mutually exclusive groups: PTB+, PTB-U, and non-TB. PTB+ was defined as pulmonary tuberculosis with bacteriological confirmation by culture, Xpert MTB/RIF or Xpert Ultra, or another accepted microbiological method on a respiratory specimen. PTB-U was defined as pulmonary tuberculosis without bacteriological confirmation, with final diagnosis based on integrated clinical assessment incorporating symptoms, imaging, clinician judgement, follow-up information, and treatment response when relevant.5 Non-TB was defined as an alternative final diagnosis with no evidence supporting active pulmonary tuberculosis during follow-up.
Because microbiological testing was performed according to clinical indications in routine practice, not all participants underwent exactly the same microbiological work-up. We did not treat 'not tested' as equivalent to 'negative'. This heterogeneity was considered part of the intended-use clinical context rather than a protocol deviation.
The primary outcome was diagnostic performance of the locked classifier for identifying PTB-U in the intended-use population. Secondary outcomes were the strictly independent stage-2 benchmark analysis, temporal robustness analyses in stage 3, and exploratory operational and representation analyses.
Statistical analysis
All performance analyses treated the breathomics classifier as a locked system. The model architecture, preprocessing pipeline, and probability threshold were fixed after stage-1 internal validation and before any evaluation in PTB-U or in the temporal cohort.
Threshold selection followed a prespecified constrained operating-point rule. We selected the probability threshold that maximised sensitivity while maintaining specificity of at least 80%, informed by WHO target product profile considerations for non-sputum triage tests. If more than one threshold satisfied the same maximal sensitivity, we selected the threshold with the highest specificity. This report was prepared in accordance with STARD 2015 and was additionally informed by TRIPOD+AI and STARD-AI guidance for AI-enabled diagnostic-accuracy and prediction-model studies.21-23
The primary estimand was the diagnostic performance of the locked classifier for identifying PTB-U in the intended-use population. The primary analysis therefore pooled all PTB-U cases from stage 2 and stage 3 and compared them with an independent pooled non-TB control set comprising the 17 non-TB controls held out a priori from stage 1 and the 55 non-TB participants in stage 3. This pooled analysis was prespecified to preserve independence from model development while improving the precision of specificity estimation relative to the stage-2 benchmark analysis alone.
Secondary analyses included the strictly independent stage-2 benchmark analysis alone and temporal robustness analyses in stage 3. Stage-3 temporal analyses were regarded as supportive evidence only because the PTB-U subgroup in that cohort was small.
Discrimination was summarised using the area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC) with 95% confidence intervals. At the locked threshold, we report sensitivity and specificity with 95% confidence intervals; positive predictive value, negative predictive value, and likelihood ratios can be reported as supplementary metrics where relevant. The study emphasised estimation rather than formal hypothesis testing, and no multiplicity adjustment was applied because downstream analyses were prespecified as complementary rather than confirmatory tests of a single null hypothesis.
Results
Study population, data partitions, and analysis sets
Between Jan 1, 2021, and Aug 31, 2023, 703 adults with suspected pulmonary tuberculosis were screened for eligibility at Shenzhen Third People's Hospital. 697 were enrolled, and 690 had analysable breath spectra after quality control. The stage-1/2 analytic cohort comprised 652 participants: 316 PTB+, 166 PTB-U, and 170 non-TB.
For stage-1 model development, only PTB+ and non-TB participants from the stage-1/2 analytic cohort were used. These 486 participants were split into 389 participants in the training set (253 PTB+ and 136 non-TB) and 80 participants in the internal validation set (63 PTB+ and 17 non-TB), with an additional 17 non-TB controls held out a priori for stage 2.
Stage 2 evaluated the locked classifier in the intended-use population of PTB-U and included 166 PTB-U cases from the stage-1/2 analytic cohort and the 17 held-out non-TB controls. This stage-2 comparison served as the strictly independent benchmark rather than the primary intended-use analysis.
In the prespecified primary intended-use framework, all PTB-U cases from stage 2 and stage 3 were pooled, yielding 174 PTB-U cases. The independent pooled non-TB comparison set comprised the 17 held-out non-TB controls from stage 2 and 55 non-TB participants from stage 3, for a total of 72 non-TB controls. The prespecified stage-3 temporal cohort, enrolled after model locking, comprised 87 participants: 24 PTB+, eight PTB-U, and 55 non-TB.
Stage-1 model development and threshold locking
In stage 1, the classifier was developed to distinguish PTB+ from non-TB controls using the development subset described above. In the internal validation set, the locked operating point yielded an AUC of 0.876, sensitivity of 92.1%, and specificity of 88.2%.
This operating point was selected before any PTB-U or temporal evaluation. Once selected, the model parameters, preprocessing pipeline, and threshold were not modified further.
Primary validation in bacteriologically unconfirmed pulmonary tuberculosis
The primary analysis evaluated the performance of the locked classifier for identifying PTB-U in the intended-use population, defined prespecifically as all PTB-U cases from stage 2 and stage 3 versus the pooled independent non-TB control set.

This prespecified pooled evaluation preserved independence from model development while providing a more precise estimate of specificity than the strictly independent stage-2 benchmark analysis alone.
Strictly independent benchmark evaluation in stage 2
To provide a strictly independent benchmark, we separately evaluated the locked classifier in stage 2, in which 166 PTB-U cases were compared with the 17 non-TB controls held out a priori from stage-1 development.
In this benchmark analysis, the classifier achieved an AUC of 0.841 (95% CI 0.765-0.915), sensitivity of 86.1% (95% CI 80.1-90.6), and specificity of 82.3% (95% CI 58.9-93.8) at the locked threshold.
These findings show that the signal observed in PTB+ during development remained detectable in bacteriologically unconfirmed disease under a strictly independent comparison. However, the confidence interval for specificity was wide, reflecting the small number of held-out controls in this benchmark dataset.

Exploratory analyses
Breath collection and analysis were operationally feasible in routine care. Of the 697 enrolled participants who provided breath samples, 690 (99.0%) passed spectral quality control and entered downstream analysis.
Exploratory subgroup analyses did not identify clear qualitative departures from the overall performance pattern, but these analyses were underpowered and should be interpreted cautiously.
Exploratory representation analyses suggested partial overlap between PTB+ and PTB-U breathomic patterns relative to non-TB controls, consistent with the possibility of shared disease-related signal. These analyses were hypothesis-generating only and were not used to support the primary claim of diagnostic performance.
Discussion
In this prospective diagnostic-accuracy study, a breathomics classifier developed in bacteriologically confirmed pulmonary tuberculosis retained discriminatory performance when applied, without retraining or recalibration, to bacteriologically unconfirmed pulmonary tuberculosis. The principal contribution of the study is therefore not simply that breathomics distinguished tuberculosis from non-tuberculosis controls, but that a locked classifier trained in PTB+ remained usable in the clinically relevant PTB-U population for which a non-sputum triage tool would matter most.
This distinction is clinically important. In routine practice, PTB-U is encountered when sputum is unavailable, when repeated microbiological tests remain negative despite persistent clinical suspicion, or when radiological findings and overall presentation compel clinicians to make decisions before bacteriological confirmation can be obtained. These are precisely the circumstances in which non-sputum triage information could be useful. The purpose of such a tool is not to replace microbiological confirmation, but to help prioritise further testing, closer clinical review, and timely escalation of diagnostic effort.
Several features of the present study strengthen that interpretation. First, the study was prospective and embedded in routine hospital care rather than performed in an artificial case-control environment alone. Second, the model was treated as a locked system after stage-1 internal validation, with no retraining, recalibration, or threshold modification in PTB-U or in the temporal cohort. Third, the validation strategy deliberately combined a primary intended-use analysis, a strictly independent benchmark analysis, and a prespecified temporal cohort, allowing us to separate signal persistence from any single favourable partition of the data.
The study also has conceptual relevance in light of evolving WHO terminology and policy frameworks.1,2 Patients without bacteriological confirmation are not a residual or secondary category; they are a major part of real-world tuberculosis practice.1,5 Validation in PTB-U is therefore more clinically informative than repeated demonstrations of discrimination only in bacteriologically confirmed disease.1,10-13 Heterogeneity in microbiological work-up is not merely a nuisance in this context; it is part of the clinical reality that a non-sputum triage test would have to face.6
The findings should nevertheless be interpreted within clear limits. This was a single-centre study at a tertiary tuberculosis referral hospital, and the case mix, disease prevalence, and diagnostic workflows might differ from those of secondary hospitals, primary care facilities, or community-based screening settings. The stage-2 benchmark analysis used only 17 held-out non-TB controls, which limited precision of specificity estimates. The stage-3 PTB-U subgroup was small, so temporal analyses in unconfirmed disease provide supportive rather than definitive evidence. In addition, the reference-standard classification for PTB-U was clinically grounded but imperfect, as is unavoidable in this population because final diagnosis often depends on composite judgement rather than a single definitive microbiological criterion.5
These limitations define the next steps. Multicentre external validation is needed across broader epidemiological and operational settings, including sites with different prevalence spectra and different diagnostic pathways. Further work should assess cross-site calibration and threshold transport, compare performance across specimen-constrained subgroups, and test whether adding breathomics to routine care changes downstream microbiological yield, time to diagnosis, antibiotic exposure, or empirical treatment patterns. Prospective pathway and health-economic studies will also be needed before broad implementation can be justified.
In conclusion, a breathomics classifier trained in bacteriologically confirmed pulmonary tuberculosis could be locked prospectively and applied without retraining or recalibration to bacteriologically unconfirmed pulmonary tuberculosis. The present findings suggest that breathomics might serve as a feasible hospital-based non-sputum triage approach in a tertiary-care setting, while also making clear that microbiological confirmation remains essential and that broader implementation requires external validation and pathway-level evidence.
