## Abstract

### Background

Tuberculosis and diabetes mellitus frequently coexist and are associated with higher bacteriological burden, more complicated comorbidity management, and greater post-treatment recurrence risk. Applying a single fixed treatment duration to all adults with tuberculosis and diabetes mellitus may therefore lead to overtreatment in some patients and undertreatment in others. The TDSC study evaluates whether treatment can be shortened safely in selected lower-risk patients and whether treatment may be stopped at month 6 in selected higher-risk patients who satisfy a predefined microbiological stopping gate.

### Methods

TDSC is a multicenter, open-label, stratification-first randomized controlled trial in adults with drug-susceptible pulmonary tuberculosis and type 2 diabetes mellitus. At baseline, participants are assigned to a low-treatment-difficulty cohort or a high-treatment-difficulty cohort using a rule-based stratification tool centered on bacteriological burden and imaging severity. In cohort A, participants are randomized 1:1:1 to one of two 4-month rifapentine-containing regimens or to the 6-month standard regimen. In cohort B, all participants receive standard treatment to month 6; those who satisfy a predefined microbiological Hard Gate are randomized 1:1 to stop treatment at 6 months or continue HR to 9 months. Participants who do not satisfy the Hard Gate are not randomized but continue on protocol-specified treatment and follow-up pathways with the same intensity of endpoint ascertainment. The primary efficacy endpoint is relapse-free cure at 12 months after treatment cessation. Key endpoint events are adjudicated by an independent blinded clinical endpoint committee. All participants are followed for 24 months after treatment cessation.

### Discussion

This study is designed to generate operationally implementable evidence for individualized treatment-duration optimization in adults with tuberculosis and diabetes mellitus. The protocol emphasizes auditable decision rules, standardized visit windows, event-triggered assessments, centralized endpoint adjudication, and site-friendly execution procedures.

## Introduction

### Background and rationale

Tuberculosis and diabetes mellitus increasingly overlap in high-burden settings. Diabetes mellitus is associated with greater mycobacterial burden, more complex treatment trajectories, slower treatment response, and higher post-treatment recurrence risk. These features suggest that a uniform treatment duration may not be optimal for all adults with tuberculosis and diabetes mellitus.

Evidence supporting shortened regimens for drug-susceptible tuberculosis has expanded, especially for rifapentine-containing regimens. However, existing evidence does not directly establish whether shortened strategies can be applied safely and effectively to adults with tuberculosis and diabetes mellitus. It also does not clearly define which higher-risk patients may safely stop treatment at 6 months rather than continue to 9 months.

The TDSC protocol therefore addresses two linked clinical questions within one stratification-first framework. First, among participants considered to have lower treatment difficulty at baseline, the study evaluates whether a fixed 4-month rifapentine-containing regimen is non-inferior to the standard 6-month regimen. Second, among participants considered to have higher treatment difficulty, the study evaluates whether a predefined month-6 microbiological Hard Gate can identify patients in whom treatment may safely stop at month 6. To improve reproducibility and multicenter scalability, treatment-changing decisions are anchored to standardized clinical, microbiological, and imaging-based rules, whereas candidate biomarkers and predictive models are reserved for prespecified validation and exploratory analyses.

### Objectives

#### Primary objectives

1. To determine, in cohort A, whether each of two 4-month rifapentine-containing regimens is non-inferior to the standard 6-month regimen with respect to relapse-free cure at 12 months after treatment cessation.
2. To determine, in cohort B, whether stopping treatment at 6 months in participants who satisfy the predefined Hard Gate is non-inferior to continuing treatment to 9 months with respect to relapse-free cure at 12 months after treatment cessation.

#### Secondary objectives

1. To compare relapse-free cure at 24 months after treatment cessation across treatment strategies.
2. To compare time to relapse, treatment failure, and reinfection-related outcomes.
3. To compare serious adverse events, grade 3 or higher adverse events, adverse events of special interest, and permanent treatment modifications due to adverse events.
4. To describe Hard Gate coverage, reasons for non-qualification, and downstream treatment pathways in cohort B.

#### Exploratory objectives

1. To evaluate the predictive performance and incremental value of candidate biomarkers, including TB22, CRISPR TB-DNA, and breathomics, for post-treatment recurrence.
2. To evaluate the supportive role of DSS, imaging AI, and related models in treatment-difficulty stratification.
3. To explore associations between pharmacokinetic exposure, glycemic control, and efficacy or safety outcomes.

### Trial design

TDSC is a multicenter, prospective, open-label, stratification-first randomized controlled trial. Participants are first allocated to one of two predefined cohorts according to treatment difficulty. Each cohort addresses a distinct but related treatment-optimization question within a shared framework for screening, follow-up, endpoint adjudication, safety oversight, and data management.

In cohort A, participants with low treatment difficulty are randomized 1:1:1 to Arm A1, Arm A2, or Arm A3. In cohort B, participants with high treatment difficulty receive standard treatment to month 6. Those who meet the predefined microbiological Hard Gate are randomized 1:1 to stop treatment at month 6 or continue HR to month 9. Participants who do not meet the Hard Gate are not randomized but remain on protocol-specified treatment and follow-up pathways with equal-intensity outcome tracking.

## Methods: participants, interventions, and outcomes

### Study setting and site-readiness requirements

The trial is conducted at multiple centers with established capacity for tuberculosis diagnosis and management, diabetes care, microbiological testing, imaging evaluation, and clinical trial implementation. The coordinating center is Shenzhen Third People's Hospital.

Participating sites are expected to have the capacity to:

- perform screening, enrolment, treatment delivery, and protocol follow-up in compliance with GCP;
- collect, transport, and document sputum and other protocol-required specimens;
- perform routine chest radiography, with chest CT available as a supplementary tool;
- use the electronic data capture and randomization system and respond to centralized quality control activities;
- provide site-level investigator leadership, study coordination, and data-entry support.

### Target population

The target population is adults with drug-susceptible pulmonary tuberculosis and type 2 diabetes mellitus. The study uses a reproducible treatment-difficulty stratification strategy to separate lower-risk and higher-risk groups for distinct treatment-duration questions.

### Screening pathway and informed consent

#### Screening pathway

The typical screening sequence is as follows:

1. Identify potentially eligible participants and confirm broad disease scope.
2. Obtain written informed consent before any study-specific procedures.
3. Confirm tuberculosis and diabetes diagnoses, microbiological evidence, and baseline imaging findings.
4. Complete safety laboratory testing, electrocardiography, and pregnancy assessment where applicable.
5. Apply the official stratification tool to assign the participant to cohort A or cohort B.
6. Proceed to the relevant randomization step or treatment pathway once eligibility is confirmed.

#### Informed consent

Informed consent will be obtained by the investigator or authorized study staff who have completed protocol- and site-specific training. The consent discussion will cover study purpose, expected treatment and follow-up, foreseeable risks and benefits, treatment alternatives, confidentiality protections, compensation arrangements where applicable, and the participant's right to withdraw. Additional explanation aids may be used when needed.

### Eligibility criteria

#### Inclusion criteria

1. Written informed consent before any trial-specific procedures.
2. Age 18 to 70 years at consent.
3. Type 2 diabetes mellitus diagnosed according to accepted diagnostic criteria.
4. Active pulmonary tuberculosis supported by microbiological evidence, confirmation of rifamycin susceptibility, and compatible imaging findings; pulmonary disease may be accompanied by selected thoracic extrapulmonary involvement as permitted by the source protocol.
5. No prior anti-tuberculosis treatment for the current episode, or cumulative prior anti-tuberculosis treatment not exceeding 14 days.
6. Body weight between 30 kg and 90 kg at screening.
7. Negative pregnancy test in participants with reproductive potential and agreement to use effective contraception according to protocol requirements.
8. Investigator judgment that the participant is likely to complete treatment and long-term follow-up and has reliable contact information or feasible alternative follow-up arrangements.

#### Exclusion criteria

1. Known or suspected resistance to core study drugs, or inability to exclude major resistance risk before randomization.
2. Severe extrapulmonary tuberculosis requiring specialized or prolonged treatment.
3. Strong evidence for non-tuberculous mycobacterial lung disease.
4. Acute metabolic decompensation related to diabetes requiring urgent inpatient management, such as diabetic ketoacidosis or hyperosmolar hyperglycaemic state.
5. Severe chronic complications that may substantially interfere with safety assessment or adherence.
6. Clinically important hepatic, renal, hematologic, or electrolyte abnormalities beyond protocol thresholds.
7. Significant cardiac safety concerns, including major QTc prolongation or clinically important arrhythmia risk.
8. Required long-term use of drugs with major unavoidable interactions with rifamycins.
9. HIV infection, recent systemic corticosteroid exposure, active immunosuppressive or biologic therapy, or severe autoimmune disease as defined in the source protocol.
10. Other conditions judged by the investigator to materially affect safety, adherence, or endpoint assessment.
11. Pregnancy or breastfeeding.

#### Key screening notes

- Existing laboratory, imaging, and electrocardiographic results obtained within 2 weeks before enrolment may be accepted if they satisfy protocol content and timing requirements.
- Correctable abnormalities such as electrolyte disturbances or borderline electrocardiographic findings may be rechecked according to protocol or SOP before a final eligibility decision.
- Participants may not proceed to randomization until major resistance risk has been adequately excluded.

### Baseline stratification and cohort allocation

#### Official stratification principles

Baseline risk stratification determines whether a participant enters cohort A or cohort B. The official tool is an expert rule-based stratification instrument with core variables that include:

- sputum bacteriological burden or degree of microbiological positivity;
- presence of cavitation on imaging;
- radiographic extent and severity of disease.

To ensure multicenter consistency, the stratification thresholds, definitions, and review workflow should be version-locked before first enrolment and maintained as controlled study documents.

#### Role of DSS and imaging AI

DSS, imaging AI, and other candidate stratification tools may be collected in parallel for prespecified validation and comparative analyses, but they are not used for cohort allocation, randomization eligibility, or treatment-stopping decisions. Access to these exploratory variables is restricted by default for treating teams.

### Month-6 Hard Gate in cohort B and management of non-qualifying participants

#### Hard Gate definition

At month 6, all participants in cohort B undergo a uniform gate assessment. The Hard Gate is defined exclusively by microbiological criteria:

1. At least 2 negative mycobacterial cultures within the 4 months before the month-6 visit.
2. The 2 negative cultures must be at least 7 days apart.
3. No culture re-positivity may occur between months 4 and 6 before month-6 randomization.
4. If sputum cannot be obtained, induced sputum or alternative specimen collection must be performed according to the microbiology SOP.
5. Participants without evaluable culture data are treated as not meeting the Hard Gate.

#### Position of non-qualifying participants in the study

Participants who do not meet the Hard Gate are not considered screening failures or protocol deviations. Instead, they form a prespecified non-randomized continuation subgroup. These participants should:

- continue protocol-specified treatment, typically H + R to a total treatment duration of 9 months unless resistance or clinical need requires a rescue pathway;
- undergo follow-up and endpoint assessment with the same intensity as randomized participants;
- have their gate result, reason for non-qualification, downstream treatment pathway, and outcome data recorded prospectively in structured EDC fields.

### Interventions

#### General dosing principles

All study medications are administered orally once daily. Pyridoxine supplementation is used routinely to reduce the risk of peripheral neurotoxicity. Rifapentine-containing regimens should generally be taken within 1 hour after food. Weight-band dosing, formulation details, and dose-conversion rules are governed by controlled pharmacy documents.

#### Cohort A regimens

- Arm A1: 2HP1200MZ followed by 2HP1200M, total planned duration 4 months.
- Arm A2: 2HP900MZ followed by 2HP900M, total planned duration 4 months.
- Arm A3: 2HRZE followed by 4HR, total planned duration 6 months.

Randomization in cohort A occurs at baseline. The actual administered doses must be documented according to weight-band rules in the EDC system.

#### Cohort B treatment pathway

- Months 0 to 2: H + R + Z + E.
- Months 2 to 6: H + R.
- Month 6: Hard Gate assessment.
- If Hard Gate met: 1:1 randomization to treatment stop or continuation.
- Continuation arm: H + R extended to a total treatment duration of 9 months.
- If Hard Gate not met: no entry into the randomized comparison, but continuation of protocol-specified treatment and equal-intensity follow-up.

#### Concomitant care and supportive treatment

All participants receive protocol-aligned tuberculosis-diabetes comorbidity management, including glucose monitoring and optimization of anti-diabetic therapy. Standard supportive care such as symptom relief, nutritional support, and routine clinical management is permitted. Long-term systemic corticosteroids or immunosuppressive therapies should only be used when medically necessary and should be documented with rationale and risk assessment.

#### Concomitant medication management

All prescription drugs, over-the-counter drugs, herbal products, and supplements must be recorded with start and stop dates, indication, and potential relevance to study treatment. Particular attention is required for:

- drugs with major rifamycin interactions;
- drugs associated with meaningful QT prolongation;
- agents that may increase hepatotoxic risk;
- long-term treatments that may affect adherence or endpoint assessment.

#### Missed doses, make-up dosing, and adherence

Participants should take study medication at a consistent daily time where possible. If a missed dose is identified on the same day and the next scheduled dose is more than 12 hours away, 1 make-up dose may be taken that day. If the next scheduled dose is within 12 hours, the missed dose should be skipped and double dosing is not permitted. Missed doses, make-up doses, interruptions, and permanent regimen changes must be recorded in the dosing log or electronic adherence system.

### Dose modification, interruption, and rescue therapy

#### General principles

Drug-management decisions follow the priority order of participant safety first, tuberculosis control second, and study continuity third. All changes should be documented with triggering event, DAIDS grade, management action, recovery time, and outcome.

#### Common trigger scenarios

- Hepatotoxicity: if prespecified thresholds are met, all suspected hepatotoxic drugs should be interrupted and core drugs reintroduced stepwise according to SOP after recovery.
- Ethambutol-related optic toxicity: during periods of ethambutol exposure, vision should be monitored at least monthly; if visual decline, color-vision abnormality, or visual-field defect occurs, ethambutol should be stopped immediately and ophthalmologic assessment arranged.
- QTcF abnormality: if QTcF exceeds 500 ms or increases by more than 60 ms from baseline, moxifloxacin should be interrupted and reversible causes such as hypokalaemia and interacting drugs corrected.
- Life-threatening hypersensitivity or other unacceptable risk: the suspected drug should be permanently discontinued and rescue treatment initiated.

#### Rescue therapy

Rescue treatment or protocol conversion should be initiated if any of the following occurs:

1. Further drug-susceptibility or genotypic testing confirms resistance to a core drug after randomization.
2. Bacteriological re-positivity, clear treatment failure, or clinical or radiological progression occurs during treatment after adherence problems have been considered.
3. A severe adverse event prevents continued use of key regimen components.
4. A serious extrapulmonary manifestation emerges that requires a guideline-based treatment change.

The study team should make every effort to retain follow-up, and the event should be submitted to the CEC for outcome adjudication when applicable.

#### Study-drug accountability

Study drugs are provided without charge. Procurement, storage, dispensing, return, and destruction should comply with GCP and institutional pharmacy standards, with full accountability logs maintained. At each dispensing visit, the site should verify participant identity, dosing schedule, quantity dispensed, and the interval to the next visit. Returned medication and unused tablets should be reconciled wherever applicable.

### Outcomes

#### Primary outcome

The primary efficacy outcome is relapse-free cure at 12 months after treatment cessation. The primary endpoint uses a composite unfavorable-outcome logic, and the occurrence of any component event constitutes primary endpoint failure:

1. treatment failure;
2. post-treatment relapse;
3. death from any cause;
4. efficacy-driven permanent change to the anti-tuberculosis regimen.

#### Key secondary outcomes

- relapse-free cure at 24 months after treatment cessation;
- time to relapse;
- reinfection-related outcomes;
- serious adverse events, grade 3 or higher adverse events, and adverse events of special interest;
- permanent treatment changes due to inadequate efficacy or toxicity;
- Hard Gate coverage, non-qualification reasons, and downstream pathways in cohort B.

#### Uniform endpoint-adjudication rules

- microbiological confirmation takes priority over composite clinical evidence;
- if multiple event definitions are met, the earliest qualifying event date is used as the primary endpoint-failure date;
- if retreatment has been started on strong clinical grounds, the event must still be submitted for CEC adjudication even if culture confirmation is pending;
- events ultimately attributable to contamination or non-tuberculous mycobacteria are not counted as relapse or failure, but their effect on treatment decisions must still be recorded;
- all deaths count toward the primary composite unfavorable outcome regardless of final cause-of-death attribution.

### Participant timeline and visit framework

#### Overall timeline

The standard visit schedule includes:

- baseline and randomization (W0);
- weeks 2, 4, 8, and 12;
- week 16 as the end-of-treatment window for 4-month regimens;
- week 20;
- week 24 or month 6 as the end-of-treatment window for 6-month regimens and the Hard Gate and randomization window for cohort B;
- weeks 28 and 32;
- week 36 or month 9 as the end-of-treatment window for 9-month regimens;
- post-treatment visits at 3, 6, 9, 12, 18, and 24 months after treatment cessation.

All participants continue follow-up for 24 months after their own treatment end date. Total observation time is therefore approximately 28 months, 30 months, or 33 months depending on treatment duration.

#### Recommended visit windows

- W2, W4, and W8: plus or minus 3 days.
- W12, W16, W20, W24, W28, W32, and W36: plus or minus 7 days.
- Post-treatment months 3, 6, 9, 12, 18, and 24: plus or minus 14 days.

Suspected relapse, serious adverse events, or metabolic crises should trigger unscheduled assessments and are not restricted by routine visit windows.

#### Core assessments by stage

Core screening and baseline assessments include:

- informed consent;
- eligibility confirmation;
- tuberculosis and diabetes diagnostic confirmation;
- smear, culture, Xpert or Xpert Ultra, and other required microbiology;
- baseline chest radiograph, with chest CT when clinically or operationally relevant;
- liver function, renal function, hematology, electrolytes, urinalysis, and required infection screening;
- electrocardiography, pregnancy assessment, and HbA1c;
- treatment-difficulty stratification, cohort allocation, and baseline randomization preparation.

Core treatment-period assessments include:

- adherence review and drug dispensing or reconciliation;
- symptom review, physical status, and tuberculosis-related event screening;
- AE, SAE, and AESI assessment;
- key laboratory and electrocardiographic monitoring;
- sputum culture and molecular testing;
- concomitant medication review and anti-diabetic treatment changes.

Core end-of-treatment assessments include:

- confirmation of treatment-stop date;
- summary of efficacy status and safety status;
- review of key microbiological and imaging evidence;
- preparation for post-treatment follow-up and contact reconfirmation.

Core post-treatment assessments include:

- relapse-related symptoms and retreatment status;
- survival status;
- available microbiological and imaging results;
- major safety events and relevant comorbidity events;
- metabolic events and major medication changes.

#### Imaging principles

Chest radiography is the principal imaging modality for decision-making and routine follow-up. Chest CT is a supplementary assessment tool. Both may be used in parallel, but completion of chest radiographs should be prioritized to support eventual implementation in lower-resource settings.

### Cohort B month-6 gate-assessment operational points

At month 6 or week 24, all cohort B participants undergo a uniform Hard Gate assessment. The following steps should be explicitly checked:

1. confirm that the gate-assessment time window is valid;
2. verify that the culture history within the preceding 4 months satisfies the requirement for at least 2 negative cultures separated by at least 7 days;
3. verify that no culture re-positivity occurred between months 4 and 6;
4. if sputum is unavailable, follow the SOP for induced sputum or alternative specimen collection rather than recording a simple non-submission;
5. use the EDC workflow to preserve the trigger-review-confirm sequence and the audit trail;
6. do not use imaging findings or candidate biomarkers as stopping-eligibility criteria.

### Event-triggered assessments and the minimum follow-up dataset

#### Trigger scenarios

Event-triggered assessment should be initiated immediately if any of the following occurs:

1. return of tuberculosis-related symptoms after treatment cessation, renewed positive smear, culture, or molecular testing, or imaging suggesting new or progressive disease;
2. persistent culture positivity, suspected resistance, or clear clinical or radiological progression during treatment;
3. a major safety event such as SAE, AESI, suspected drug-induced liver injury, major QTcF abnormality, severe hypoglycaemia, diabetic ketoacidosis, or hyperosmolar hyperglycaemic state.

#### Minimum follow-up dataset

For missed visits, remote follow-up, or passive tracing, the site should attempt to obtain the following minimum dataset so that the primary endpoint can still be assessed:

- survival status;
- tuberculosis-related symptoms;
- retreatment status;
- hospitalization or emergency-visit information;
- major safety events;
- available sputum culture or molecular results;
- available imaging findings.

### Retention and loss-to-follow-up prevention

#### General principles

The study distinguishes clearly between stopping study treatment and withdrawing from the study. Participants who stop study treatment should still be followed whenever possible. Withdrawal applies only when the participant explicitly withdraws consent and refuses any further follow-up, is formally classified as lost to follow-up, dies, or the study is terminated.

#### Tiered contact workflow

If a participant has not completed a required key assessment after the visit window ends, the site should use a tiered retention workflow with documented contact logs:

1. Level 1: within the visit window or within 7 days after window closure, at least 1 telephone contact plus at least 1 text or messaging reminder.
2. Level 2: within 14 days after window closure, at least 3 cumulative telephone attempts at different times or dates plus at least 1 emergency-contact attempt.
3. Level 3: within 30 days after window closure, consider home outreach, community coordination, or facilitated local reassessment where ethically approved, and initiate passive tracing.

If the minimum follow-up dataset is obtained, the participant should not be classified as lost to follow-up and may instead be classified as having completed remote or alternative follow-up.

#### Passive tracing

Unless consent has been explicitly withdrawn, the study team may use ethically approved passive-tracing sources such as hospital records, tuberculosis registries, local reassessment reports, and structured telephone follow-up to recover endpoint-relevant information. All alternative follow-up sources must be identified in the EDC, with the supporting evidence trail retained.

### Sample size and recruitment

The planned total sample size is 1350 participants, including approximately 850 in cohort A and 500 in cohort B. Cohort A is powered for 2 non-inferiority comparisons with multiplicity control. Cohort B assumes that approximately 50% to 60% of participants will satisfy the month-6 Hard Gate and undergo randomization, yielding an estimated randomized sample of approximately 250 to 300 participants.

Participants will be recruited through tuberculosis clinics, inpatient wards, and routine care pathways at participating centers. Screening-failure reasons, gate non-qualification reasons, and non-randomization reasons should be documented in structured form to support interpretation and future implementation.

## Methods: assignment of interventions

### Sequence generation

Randomization sequences are generated centrally by independent statistical personnel using computerized permuted blocks. The block sizes are concealed from study sites. Stratification factors are locked before first enrolment and documented in the controlled randomization specification.

### Allocation concealment mechanism

Allocation is implemented through the EDC and IRT system. Treatment allocation is not released until eligibility for the relevant randomization step has been fully confirmed. The system maintains a full audit trail.

### Implementation

Participants are enrolled by site investigators or authorized study staff. Cohort A is randomized at baseline. Cohort B is randomized only at month 6 and only if the participant satisfies the Hard Gate. Participants who do not satisfy the Hard Gate enter the prespecified non-randomized continuation subgroup and remain under protocol follow-up.

### Randomization stratification factors

According to the source protocol, randomization stratification factors include at least:

- study center;
- bacteriological burden category;
- radiographic extent category;
- HbA1c stratum.

The final list remains governed by the locked randomization specification prepared before first enrolment.

### Blinding and bias control

Treatment allocation is open-label. Bias mitigation measures include:

- blinded adjudication of key endpoint events by the independent CEC;
- independent review of unblinded safety and operational indicators by the DSMB;
- centralized imaging review and arbitration where required;
- restricted access to candidate biomarkers and AI-derived scores for treating teams.

## Methods: data collection, management, and analysis

### Data sources

Study data sources include medical records, outpatient and inpatient records, laboratory information systems, imaging archives, electrocardiography systems, pharmacy dispensing records, study-specific logs, participant-reported outcome systems, and central or reference laboratory outputs. All data must have traceable source documentation or equivalent electronic source data.

### EDC and IRT architecture

The study uses an electronic data capture system as the main data repository, linked where applicable to the randomization and drug-assignment system. The EDC should support permission-based access, audit trails, edit checks, query management, data export, and version control. System changes should follow a controlled change-management process to preserve data integrity.

### eCRF design principles

The eCRF is built around the study workflow and endpoint framework and follows a minimum-necessary-dataset principle. Priority data fields include:

- eligibility;
- stratification and randomization;
- Hard Gate assessment;
- primary endpoint component events;
- serious adverse events, grade 3 or higher adverse events, and AESI;
- study-drug exposure and permanent regimen changes.

### Data-entry timelines

To maintain timeliness, sites should follow these entry timelines:

- routine visit data within 3 calendar days after visit completion;
- critical events within 24 hours of awareness for the initial report and key fields;
- laboratory, electrocardiographic, and imaging results within 7 calendar days after report availability.

New staff must complete EDC and data-standard training before receiving system access.

### Query management and pre-lock cleaning

The EDC should include edit checks for range values, cross-form consistency, time logic, visit windows, and critical missing variables. Query management follows a closed-loop principle, with each query resolved by correction, explanation, or documented inability to correct. Suggested response timelines are:

- general queries within 10 working days;
- critical-data queries within 5 working days;
- endpoint- and safety-related queries within 48 hours.

Pre-lock cleaning should focus on missing critical variables, outliers, endpoint-event timelines, consistency between randomization logs and dispensing records, consistency between SAE and AE datasets, and agreement between CEC adjudication results and endpoint classification.

### Statistical methods

The trial uses a non-inferiority framework. Cohort A evaluates A1 versus A3 and A2 versus A3 with multiplicity control. Cohort B evaluates the month-6 stopping strategy versus continuation to month 9 among randomized Hard Gate-qualified participants, with a non-inferiority margin of 10% according to the source protocol. Primary analysis populations, missing-data handling, sensitivity analyses, and secondary analyses will be prespecified in the statistical analysis plan.

### Interim analysis

One formal interim analysis is planned when approximately 50% of participants contributing to the primary efficacy analysis have completed the primary endpoint window. The interim review will use an O'Brien-Fleming-type alpha-spending approach. According to the source protocol, cohort A may stop further A1 enrolment after interim review if recommended by the DSMB.

## Methods: monitoring and quality control

### Critical data and critical processes

Quality management is centered on critical data and critical processes. Critical data include eligibility, stratification, randomization, Hard Gate status, primary endpoint components, key safety endpoints, and drug exposure. Critical processes include screening and enrolment, randomization, gate assessment, key specimen and imaging collection, endpoint-event identification, AE reporting, and database lock.

### Project management office and site responsibilities

The project management office is responsible for site initiation, training, material and document control, EDC configuration and access management, enrolment and follow-up tracking, issue and risk logs, and cross-center coordination. Participating sites are expected to:

- designate a Site PI, CRC or study nurse, and data-entry staff;
- complete screening, consent, randomization, treatment, visits, specimen collection, AE reporting, and source-data maintenance according to unified SOPs;
- respond to monitoring findings, data queries, and corrective actions.

### Data and safety monitoring board

The DSMB is independent of the study-execution team and periodically reviews cumulative safety data, key operational indicators, and selected efficacy signals when applicable. Detailed conduct is governed by the DSMB charter.

### Clinical endpoint committee

The CEC adjudicates treatment failure, relapse, reinfection, death, and efficacy-driven permanent regimen changes in a blinded and independent manner. Adjudication sources include microbiology, imaging, clinical documentation, and strain typing or whole-genome sequencing where available.

### Microbiology and imaging quality control

#### Microbiology

Specimen collection, transport, culture interpretation, contamination management, and non-tuberculous mycobacterial exclusion follow unified SOPs. For re-positivity or suspected failure events, original reports and organism-identification results must be assembled into the CEC evidence package.

#### Imaging

Chest radiographs and CT scans should follow standardized de-identification and upload procedures. Key-node imaging should undergo centralized review, sampling checks, and consistency calibration where required. CT remains supplementary and does not replace chest radiography as the principal decision-making image.

### Risk-based monitoring

The study uses an RBM and RBQM approach combining remote monitoring, centralized statistical monitoring, and on-site verification where needed. Key quality indicators may include:

- enrolment and randomization compliance;
- critical-field missingness;
- query turnaround times;
- visit-window deviations;
- completion rates for culture and chest radiography;
- SAE reporting timeliness;
- protocol-deviation frequency.

Outlier sites may trigger directed monitoring, retraining, or CAPA procedures.

### Training and consistency maintenance

Training should cover the study protocol, key SOPs, informed consent, randomization and EDC workflow, microbiology and imaging workflow, AE identification and DAIDS grading, and data-entry and query-response standards. Attendance records, competency checks, and retraining documentation should be retained.

## Ethics and dissemination

### Safety-governance framework

Safety management uses a layered governance structure:

- investigators identify, grade, attribute, manage, and initially report AEs;
- the project management office oversees timelines, trend summaries, and cross-site coordination;
- the data-management team maintains AE modules, auto-grading rules, and audit trails;
- a medical monitor or pharmacovigilance lead, where applicable, reviews important safety signals;
- the DSMB periodically reviews cumulative safety information;
- ethics committees review the protocol and participant-protection measures.

### AE, SAE, and AESI management

#### Identification and capture

AE collection uses a combined strategy of active questioning, scheduled assessments, passive reporting, and PRO prompts. AEs should be assessed and recorded at every planned visit and unscheduled clinical contact. Symptomatic adverse events are captured through both investigator assessment and PRO-CTCAE support signals.

#### Grading and attribution

DAIDS is the sole severity-grading framework for this study. Structured laboratory and electrocardiographic data may generate automated grading prompts in the EDC, which investigators then confirm. Attribution is based on investigator clinical judgment.

#### Reporting timelines

All AEs should be entered promptly into the EDC. SAEs and selected AESI require stricter reporting timelines under regulatory, ethics, and protocol requirements. Initial reporting of critical safety events should generally occur within 24 hours of awareness.

#### AESI focus areas

Key AESI categories include:

- peripheral neuropathy and optic neuropathy;
- bone marrow suppression;
- hepatotoxicity;
- QTcF prolongation and arrhythmia;
- severe hypersensitivity and severe cutaneous adverse reactions;
- severe gastrointestinal intolerance;
- severe hypoglycaemia and hyperglycaemic crises.

### Risk-minimization measures

To reduce drug-related risk and improve cross-site consistency, the study includes:

1. baseline laboratory, electrocardiographic, and comorbidity evaluation before enrolment;
2. scheduled monitoring of liver function, hematology, electrocardiography, and vision according to the visit schedule;
3. a contraindicated and cautionary concomitant-medication framework with interaction checks;
4. routine pyridoxine supplementation to reduce peripheral neuropathy risk;
5. participant education on red-flag symptoms and site-contact pathways;
6. repeated training on DAIDS grading, SAE reporting, QTcF abnormality management, and severe rash or hypersensitivity response.

### Pregnancy, breastfeeding, and special populations

Pregnant and breastfeeding women are not eligible. If pregnancy occurs during the study, treatment exposure risk should be assessed promptly, study treatment should be stopped, and pregnancy outcome and maternal safety follow-up should continue. Pregnancy complications or adverse pregnancy outcomes should be recorded through the AE or SAE framework as applicable.

### Ethics approval and protocol amendments

The study must obtain ethics approval from the coordinating center and all participating centers before enrolment. Any substantive amendment affecting participant risk, rights, or key study procedures must be approved according to ethics and regulatory requirements. Urgent safety amendments may be implemented according to applicable regulations and then submitted promptly for review.

### Confidentiality and data security

Participant data should be managed in coded or de-identified form and stored in secure, access-controlled systems. Any dataset used for adjudication, analysis, or controlled sharing must remain within the approved ethical scope and preserve access logs and audit trails.

### Dissemination policy

Study results are intended for dissemination through peer-reviewed publications, scientific meetings, and other scientific communications. Authorship, conflict-of-interest disclosure, and results reporting should follow ICMJE and journal standards.

## Discussion

This execution-enhanced English version preserves the Trials and SPIRIT-aligned structure while restoring the operational details that site investigators and coordinators need for implementation. For multicenter use, it is not sufficient to describe only the scientific question; sites also need a clear answer to when to screen, how to stratify, how to verify the Hard Gate, when to trigger event reporting, and how to retain endpoint follow-up after treatment stops. This version therefore reincorporates visit windows, gate-assessment rules, minimum follow-up datasets, retention tiers, AE-handling principles, and site responsibilities from the operational source material.

The study still reflects a pragmatic open-label design. However, central randomization, blinded endpoint adjudication, independent DSMB review, unified SOPs, RBQM, and centralized data-quality control together provide a framework intended to reduce bias and improve reliability in a multicenter real-world environment.
