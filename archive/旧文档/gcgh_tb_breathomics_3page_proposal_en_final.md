# A PTR-TOFMS Breathomics Platform for Non-Sputum Tuberculosis Screening and Near-Patient Triage in LMIC Settings

## 1. Introductory Information

Tuberculosis remains a leading infectious cause of death, and the diagnostic gap is greatest in low- and middle-income countries (LMICs). A major reason is continued dependence on sputum and laboratory infrastructure. In routine care, many patients cannot produce sputum, produce poor-quality sputum, or remain bacteriologically unconfirmed despite imaging and clinical findings that strongly suggest pulmonary tuberculosis (PTB). This problem is especially important in bacteriologically unconfirmed pulmonary tuberculosis (PTB-U), where uncertainty can delay diagnosis, prolong inappropriate empiric treatment, and trigger repeated work-up without definitive confirmation.

WHO target product profile directions increasingly favor non-sputum, easy-to-obtain, rapid, near-patient tools that move patients earlier into the correct diagnostic pathway. Exhaled breath is attractive in this context because sampling is non-invasive, repeatable, and operationally compatible with front-end screening and triage. However, most published breath-based TB studies remain small, case-control, or focused mainly on bacteriologically confirmed disease rather than on the intended-use populations where non-sputum tools matter most.

We propose to develop and validate a rapid, non-sputum breathomics platform based on proton-transfer-reaction time-of-flight mass spectrometry (PTR-TOFMS) for TB screening and near-patient triage in LMIC settings. Our primary intended-use population is suspected PTB patients who are sputum-scarce or bacteriologically unconfirmed, especially PTB-U. Our secondary expansion path is integration into high-risk screening algorithms together with chest X-ray/computer-aided detection (CXR/CAD) and downstream molecular testing. The tool is designed to complement microbiological confirmation, not replace it. The current project is conducted on the PTR-TOFMS platform, which has already undergone internal cross-platform bridging and is now treated as the operational platform for this translational program.

## 2. Proposal Information

Our premise is that breathomics captures a disease-state signal associated with active PTB that remains clinically informative in PTB-U, where a non-sputum triage tool would have the greatest value. We will apply this premise on a new PTR-TOFMS platform and focus the project on a high-value clinical use case rather than on platform transition itself.

We have a strong foundation for this work. Our team has built a breathomics dataset exceeding 10,000 samples. In published blinded testing, our prior TB breathomics study achieved 91.7% sensitivity, 93.0% specificity, and an AUC of 0.975 in a large PTB cohort, with useful discrimination against other pulmonary diseases. In a published diabetes comorbidity study, the platform also performed strongly in a clinically difficult subgroup in whom sputum-based diagnosis often underperforms. We additionally have prospective PTB-U bridge evidence from a locked-model diagnostic-accuracy study: a classifier developed in bacteriologically confirmed PTB retained discriminatory performance in PTB-U without retraining or recalibration, with a strictly independent benchmark AUC of 0.841, sensitivity of 86.1%, specificity of 82.3%, and a breath spectral quality-control pass rate of 99.0%. We also have a standardized breath sampling SOP covering fasting and smoking restrictions, water rinse, background-air sampling, leak checks, system blanks, environmental monitoring, and independent QC oversight.

This project is innovative in four ways. First, it targets the intended-use population rather than repeating discrimination studies in confirmed PTB alone. Second, it treats the system as a deployable non-sputum triage platform, not merely a machine-learning classifier. Third, it combines standardized sampling, locked-model validation, and workflow integration, moving beyond biomarker discovery toward implementation-informing evidence. Fourth, it focuses on LMIC-relevant operational value: fast turnaround, minimal consumables, workflow compatibility, and a credible path to lowering front-end triage costs.

The key competitive question for this call is why breathomics should be added to existing pathways rather than replaced by them. Our answer is that the platform addresses a different bottleneck. CXR/CAD provides morphological screening but does not solve the need for rapid non-sputum risk stratification at the point of first clinical contact, while molecular testing remains highly effective but still depends on sample availability and pathway completion. Breathomics is therefore positioned as a front-end complement that can help identify which patients should be prioritized immediately for intensified microbiological work-up, especially when sputum is unavailable, repeated testing is negative despite persistent suspicion, or same-day triage decisions are needed.

We will pursue three aims.

**Aim 1: Optimize the PTR-TOFMS platform and minimum viable operating package for reproducible use in real clinical settings.**  
We will finalize the deployment-ready platform definition, including patient preparation, environmental background control, sampling workflow, calibration, system blanks, acceptance criteria, QC rules, preprocessing, result output, operator training thresholds, site-readiness requirements, and maintenance routines. The goal is reproducible operation across sites and operators under routine care conditions.

**Aim 2: Prospectively validate a locked PTR-TOFMS breathomics model in real-world intended-use populations, especially PTB-U and sputum-scarce suspected PTB.**  
We will conduct multicenter, prospective, consecutive enrollment in suspected PTB populations. The model architecture, preprocessing pipeline, and operating threshold will be locked before formal evaluation. Primary analyses will focus on active PTB overall versus non-TB and on PTB-U versus non-TB, with prespecified estimates of sensitivity, specificity, and AUC. Secondary analyses will assess cross-site transportability, subgroup robustness, and supportive pathway metrics such as time to diagnostic work-up completion and downstream microbiological testing patterns.

**Aim 3: Build the LMIC deployment, workflow integration, and cost-down pathway needed to support a cost-disruptive product proposition.**  
We will define how this platform can fit into two high-value scenarios: hospital-based near-patient triage for PTB-U and sputum-constrained patients, and front-end screening for high-risk populations in conjunction with CXR/CAD or molecular testing. We will model throughput, consumables, operator burden, maintenance requirements, and cost structure, and identify the steps needed to materially reduce front-end triage costs in LMIC care pathways.

By the end of the project, we expect to deliver a standardized PTR-TOFMS TB breathomics platform package, prospective multicenter evidence in intended-use populations, and a credible deployment pathway for LMIC screening and triage workflows.

## 3. Development Plan and Path to Impact

In months 1-9, we will finalize platform operating parameters, standardize the SOP package, define QC thresholds, train participating sites, and lock the model and operating threshold. This phase will generate the technical package required for consistent multicenter implementation.

In months 10-24, we will conduct multicenter prospective enrollment in intended-use populations. Enrollment will prioritize patients who are sputum-scarce, bacteriologically unconfirmed, smear-negative despite persistent suspicion, or otherwise difficult to triage rapidly using conventional pathways alone. We will monitor analytical stability, cross-site reproducibility, quality-control pass rates, and locked-model performance without retraining or recalibration.

In months 25-36, we will complete validation, finalize workflow integration analyses, and complete deployment and cost modeling. We will identify the strongest initial implementation case for the platform and generate the evidence package needed for the next stage of external validation and pathway studies.

Key milestones will include a prespecified QC pass-rate target, stable analytical performance across sites, successful use of a locked threshold across centers, completion of a multicenter intended-use validation dataset, and delivery of a workflow and deployment package. Immediate impact will be better prioritization of suspected PTB-U and sputum-scarce patients for microbiological work-up and closer clinical evaluation. Medium-term impact will be extension into high-risk screening algorithms where rapid, non-sputum triage can improve resource allocation. Long-term impact will be a scalable breath-based respiratory diagnostics platform that can support additional LMIC use cases.

This project is timely because it is no longer a concept-stage biomarker effort. The platform signal is established, the transition to PTR-TOFMS is complete, bridge validation has been achieved, and the remaining barrier is translational: standardization, multicenter intended-use evidence, and workflow economics. At the end of this project, the platform will be positioned not only as a promising technology, but as a deployment-ready triage package with a clear next-stage pathway for external validation and implementation studies.

## 4. Cost and Manufacturing Plan

The cost logic of this project differs from consumable-heavy assay models. Our approach is based on reusable instrumentation, minimal per-sample consumables, rapid sample cycle time, and the potential to amortize capital costs across large testing volumes. The sampling workflow uses low-complexity components and limited single-use materials while avoiding reagent-intensive chemistry. This creates a credible pathway to materially lower front-end triage costs, especially where sputum pathways are delayed, fragmented, or incomplete.

We will use a staged cost logic. Phase 1 will focus on tertiary and referral-center triage, where economic value is created by reducing repeated failed sputum pathways, accelerating correct work-up, and prioritizing scarce diagnostic resources in PTB-U and sputum-constrained patients. Phase 2 will extend to higher-volume hospital and outpatient screening workflows, where increased throughput improves capital amortization. Phase 3 will define the engineering and operational requirements for lighter deployment models that can support broader decentralization.

We will model the per-test cost structure, including sampling materials, staff time, platform operation, calibration, maintenance, and utilization assumptions. We will also define the workflow conditions needed for favorable economics, such as minimum throughput, training requirements, service schedules, and integration with existing TB work-up pathways. Manufacturing and deployment planning will emphasize standardization, simplified operation, quality-control routines, and serviceability rather than laboratory-only flexibility.

A key risk is that capital and maintenance requirements could limit scalability in lower-resource settings. We address this directly by targeting the first product claim at the use cases where unmet need and clinical value are highest, quantifying throughput-driven cost reduction, and identifying the constraints that must be solved for broader LMIC deployment, including service, calibration, operator training, environment, and digital workflow integration. Our goal is not to claim immediate near-zero incremental cost at current maturity, but to provide a credible staged pathway toward cost-disruptive front-end triage in LMIC workflows.

## 5. Team and Capacity

Our team combines three capabilities rarely assembled in a single program: deep clinical TB expertise, a large breathomics data and methods foundation, and a translational device platform.

The clinical team is anchored in a high-volume TB referral center with direct access to suspected PTB, PTB-U, and relevant non-TB comparator populations. The analytical platform team has extensive experience in PTR-TOFMS operation, calibration, signal processing, standardized breath workflows, and device optimization. The data and AI team has experience in preprocessing, locked-model deployment, prospective diagnostic-accuracy evaluation, and reporting aligned with STARD and TRIPOD+AI principles. Together, the team has already produced published work in PTB, TB-DM, LTBI, and infectious-disease breathomics and has established a standardized SOP and QC infrastructure for breath sampling and analysis.

We also have the translational readiness required for this project. We have completed the transition to the PTR-TOFMS platform, built a large historical breath dataset, demonstrated bridge evidence in PTB-U, and established an operational workflow that supports multicenter deployment. The project therefore starts from a mature translational base rather than from an exploratory stage. Few groups combine a clinically meaningful intended-use focus, a large breathomics evidence base, a deployable mass-spectrometry platform, and prospective PTB-U bridge evidence. This combination makes our team well positioned not only to publish strong data, but to deliver the standardized platform package, intended-use validation, and LMIC deployment pathway proposed here.
