## Abstract

### Background

Tuberculosis and diabetes mellitus frequently coexist and are associated with substantial heterogeneity in clinical presentation, treatment response, and risk of recurrence. In adults with tuberculosis and diabetes mellitus (TB-DM), a uniform treatment duration may lead to overtreatment in patients with a relatively low treatment difficulty and undertreatment in those with a higher treatment difficulty. The TDSC study is designed to evaluate two related clinical questions within one stratification-first trial framework.

### Methods

TDSC is a multicenter, open-label, randomized controlled trial using a stratification-first design. At baseline, adults with drug-susceptible pulmonary tuberculosis and type 2 diabetes mellitus are assigned to a low-treatment-difficulty cohort or a high-treatment-difficulty cohort using a rule-based stratification tool based primarily on bacteriological burden and chest imaging severity. In cohort A, participants judged to be at low treatment difficulty are randomized 1:1:1 to one of two 4-month rifapentine-containing regimens or to the 6-month standard regimen (2HRZE/4HR). In cohort B, participants judged to be at high treatment difficulty receive uniform treatment to month 6; those who meet a predefined microbiological Hard Gate are randomized 1:1 to stop treatment at 6 months or continue HR to a total treatment duration of 9 months. The primary efficacy endpoint is relapse-free cure at 12 months after treatment cessation. Key endpoint events are adjudicated by an independent blinded clinical endpoint committee. Participants are followed for 24 months after treatment cessation.

### Discussion

This trial aims to generate operationally useful and methodologically robust evidence for treatment-duration optimization in adults with TB-DM. By restricting treatment-changing decisions to standardized and auditable microbiological and imaging-based assessments, while reserving biomarkers and prediction tools for prespecified validation analyses, the study seeks to balance scientific rigor, reproducibility, and real-world implementability.

## Background

Tuberculosis and diabetes mellitus are increasingly overlapping epidemics in many high-burden settings. Diabetes mellitus is associated with a higher risk of developing active tuberculosis, a greater mycobacterial burden, more complex treatment trajectories, worse treatment outcomes, and an increased risk of post-treatment recurrence. In this context, applying a single fixed treatment duration across all patients with TB-DM may be clinically suboptimal.

Recent randomized evidence has supported shortened treatment regimens for drug-susceptible tuberculosis in selected populations, particularly regimens incorporating rifapentine. However, the available evidence does not directly establish whether these shortened strategies can be applied safely and effectively to adults with TB-DM. Nor does it adequately define which patients at higher baseline treatment difficulty may safely discontinue treatment after 6 months.

The TB-DM population is highly heterogeneous with respect to bacteriological burden, radiological severity, pace of microbiological response, complexity of comorbidity management, and risk of relapse after treatment completion. If treatment allocation or stopping decisions rely on complex, poorly reproducible, or resource-intensive variables, the resulting strategy may have limited scalability across centers and health systems. Accordingly, the TDSC protocol prioritizes standardized clinical, microbiological, and imaging-based variables for decision-making and reserves candidate biomarkers and predictive models for prespecified validation and exploratory analyses.

The overall design addresses two linked clinical questions. First, in participants considered at lower treatment difficulty, the study evaluates whether a fixed 4-month rifapentine-containing regimen can maintain acceptable efficacy and safety compared with the standard 6-month regimen. Second, in participants considered at higher treatment difficulty, the study evaluates whether treatment can be safely stopped at month 6 when a prespecified microbiological Hard Gate has been met, rather than being routinely extended to 9 months. The protocol is intended to generate evidence that is not only scientifically credible but also operationally actionable in multicenter practice.

## Objectives

### Primary objectives

1. To determine, in cohort A, whether each of two 4-month rifapentine-containing regimens is non-inferior to the standard 6-month regimen with respect to relapse-free cure at 12 months after treatment cessation.
2. To determine, in cohort B, whether stopping treatment at 6 months in participants who meet the prespecified Hard Gate is non-inferior to continuing treatment to 9 months with respect to relapse-free cure at 12 months after treatment cessation.

### Secondary objectives

1. To compare relapse-free cure at 24 months after treatment cessation across treatment strategies.
2. To compare time to relapse, treatment failure, and reinfection-related outcomes.
3. To compare serious adverse events, grade 3 or higher adverse events, adverse events of special interest, and treatment modifications or permanent discontinuations due to adverse events.
4. To describe the coverage, failure patterns, and downstream clinical pathways associated with the month 6 Hard Gate in cohort B.

### Exploratory objectives

1. To evaluate the predictive performance and incremental value of candidate biomarkers, including TB22, CRISPR TB-DNA, and breathomics, for post-treatment recurrence.
2. To evaluate the potential supportive role of DSS, imaging AI, and related models in treatment-difficulty stratification.
3. To explore associations between pharmacokinetic exposure, glycemic control measures including HbA1c, and efficacy or safety outcomes.

## Trial design

TDSC is a multicenter, prospective, open-label, randomized controlled trial using a stratification-first design. Participants are first allocated to one of two predefined cohorts based on treatment difficulty. Each cohort addresses a distinct but clinically related treatment-optimization question within a shared framework for screening, follow-up, endpoint adjudication, data capture, and monitoring.

### Cohort A

Cohort A includes participants classified at baseline as having low treatment difficulty. Participants are randomized in a 1:1:1 ratio to:

- Arm A1: 2HP1200MZ/2HP1200M for 4 months
- Arm A2: 2HP900MZ/2HP900M for 4 months
- Arm A3: standard 2HRZE/4HR for 6 months

The main estimands in cohort A compare A1 versus A3 and A2 versus A3 in a non-inferiority framework.

### Cohort B

Cohort B includes participants classified at baseline as having high treatment difficulty. All participants initially receive standard therapy to month 6. At month 6, participants who satisfy the prespecified microbiological Hard Gate are randomized 1:1 to:

- Stop treatment at 6 months
- Continue HR to a total treatment duration of 9 months

Participants who do not meet the Hard Gate are not randomized, but continue on protocol-specified care pathways and undergo the same intensity of follow-up.

## Methods

### Study setting

The trial is conducted across multiple centers with established capacity for tuberculosis diagnosis and management, diabetes care, microbiological testing, imaging evaluation, and clinical trial implementation. The coordinating center is Shenzhen Third People’s Hospital.

### Eligibility criteria

#### Inclusion criteria

Participants must meet all of the following criteria:

1. Written informed consent provided before any trial-specific procedures.
2. Age 18 to 70 years at the time of consent.
3. Type 2 diabetes mellitus diagnosed according to WHO or ADA criteria.
4. Active pulmonary tuberculosis supported by microbiological evidence, confirmation of rifamycin susceptibility, and compatible imaging findings; pulmonary disease with or without selected thoracic extrapulmonary involvement as specified in the source protocol.
5. No prior anti-tuberculosis treatment for the current episode, or cumulative prior anti-tuberculosis treatment not exceeding 14 days.
6. Body weight between 30 kg and 90 kg at screening.
7. Negative pregnancy test in participants with reproductive potential and agreement to use effective contraception according to protocol requirements.
8. Investigator judgment that the participant is capable of completing treatment and long-term follow-up and has reliable means of contact.

#### Exclusion criteria

Participants meeting any of the following criteria are excluded:

1. Known or suspected resistance to core study drugs, or inability to exclude major resistance risk before randomization.
2. Severe extrapulmonary tuberculosis requiring specialized or prolonged treatment.
3. Strong evidence for non-tuberculous mycobacterial lung disease.
4. Acute metabolic decompensation related to diabetes requiring urgent inpatient management, including diabetic ketoacidosis or hyperosmolar hyperglycemic state.
5. Severe chronic complications that may substantially interfere with safety assessment or adherence, including major diabetic neuropathy or advanced diabetic retinopathy.
6. Laboratory abnormalities exceeding protocol safety thresholds, including clinically important hepatic, renal, hematologic, or electrolyte abnormalities.
7. Significant cardiac safety concerns, including marked QTc prolongation or clinically important arrhythmia risk.
8. Required long-term use of drugs with major and unavoidable interactions with rifamycins.
9. HIV infection, recent systemic corticosteroid exposure, active use of immunosuppressive or biologic therapies, or severe autoimmune disease as defined in the source protocol.
10. Other conditions judged by the investigator to materially affect safety, adherence, or endpoint assessment.
11. Pregnancy or breastfeeding.

### Baseline stratification and cohort allocation

Participants are assigned at baseline to cohort A or cohort B using an expert rule-based stratification tool. The official stratification variables are centered on bacteriological burden and imaging severity. DSS and imaging AI are collected in parallel but are not used for official cohort allocation.

### Interventions

#### General treatment principles

All study medications are administered orally once daily. Pyridoxine supplementation is used routinely to reduce the risk of peripheral neurotoxicity. Rifapentine-containing regimens are intended to be taken within 1 hour after food. Weight-band dosing, formulation details, and dose-conversion rules are specified in the pharmacy manual and associated controlled documents.

#### Cohort A regimens

- A1: 2 months of H + high-dose rifapentine 1200 mg/day + M + Z, followed by 2 months of H + rifapentine 1200 mg/day + M
- A2: 2 months of H + rifapentine 900 mg/day + M + Z, followed by 2 months of H + rifapentine 900 mg/day + M
- A3: 2HRZE followed by 4HR

#### Cohort B pathway

- Months 0-2: H + R + Z + E
- Months 2-6: H + R
- Month 6: Hard Gate assessment
- If Hard Gate met: randomization to stop at month 6 or continue HR to month 9
- If Hard Gate not met: no randomization; continuation of protocol-specified care, usually including extension of H + R to month 9 unless an alternative rescue pathway is clinically indicated

### Month 6 Hard Gate

The Hard Gate is defined exclusively by microbiological criteria:

1. At least two negative mycobacterial cultures within the 4 months before the month 6 visit.
2. The two negative cultures must be at least 7 days apart.
3. There must be no culture re-positivity between months 4 and 6 before randomization.
4. If sputum cannot be obtained, induced sputum or alternative sampling is pursued according to the microbiology SOP.
5. Participants without evaluable culture information are classified as not meeting the Hard Gate.

Imaging findings and candidate biomarkers, including TB22, are not used to determine Hard Gate eligibility.

### Concomitant care and prohibited medications

All participants receive protocol-aligned TB-DM comorbidity management, including glucose monitoring and optimization of anti-diabetic treatment. All concomitant prescription drugs, non-prescription drugs, herbal medicines, and supplements are recorded in the EDC system.

Medications that should be avoided or used with caution include:

- Drugs with major non-substitutable interactions with rifamycins
- Drugs with meaningful QT-prolonging liability
- Drugs or exposures that materially increase hepatotoxicity risk
- Other treatments that may compromise endpoint assessment or adherence

### Dose modification and rescue therapy

Dose adjustment, interruption, restart, substitution, or discontinuation follows the principle of prioritizing participant safety while preserving core anti-tuberculosis activity whenever feasible. Key scenarios include suspected hepatotoxicity, ethambutol-related optic toxicity, moxifloxacin-related QTc prolongation, severe hypersensitivity reactions, and other serious adverse events. Rescue therapy is initiated if resistance is confirmed, treatment failure is established, core protocol therapy cannot be continued, or guideline-based regimen change is clinically necessary.

### Outcomes

#### Primary efficacy outcome

Relapse-free cure at 12 months after treatment cessation.

#### Primary safety outcome

The occurrence of serious adverse events, grade 3 or higher adverse events using the DAIDS grading system, and adverse events of special interest within 52 weeks after randomization.

#### Key secondary outcomes

- Relapse-free cure at 24 months after treatment cessation
- Time to relapse
- Treatment failure
- Reinfection-related outcomes
- Safety outcomes at additional time windows, including 26 and 78 weeks
- Permanent regimen change due to inadequate efficacy
- Coverage and failure patterns of the cohort B Hard Gate

### Sample size

The planned total sample size is 1350 participants:

- Cohort A: approximately 850 participants
- Cohort B: approximately 500 participants

For cohort A, the sample size is based on two non-inferiority comparisons with multiplicity control. For cohort B, the sample size assumes that approximately 50% to 60% of participants will meet the month 6 Hard Gate and enter randomization, yielding an estimated randomized sample of approximately 250 to 300 participants and aiming for about 250 participants with usable primary-efficacy data.

### Recruitment

Participants are recruited through outpatient clinics, inpatient wards, and routine tuberculosis care pathways at participating centers. Recruitment is undertaken in accordance with local regulatory and ethics requirements. Participation is voluntary and based on informed consent.

### Allocation

#### Sequence generation

Randomization sequences are generated centrally by independent statistical personnel using computerized permuted blocks.

#### Allocation concealment mechanism

Randomization is implemented through the EDC/IRT system. Allocation is not released until all required eligibility criteria have been verified.

#### Implementation

Cohort A randomization occurs at baseline. Cohort B randomization occurs only after month 6 Hard Gate eligibility is confirmed.

#### Stratification factors

Randomization is stratified by, at minimum:

- Study center
- Bacteriological burden level
- Imaging severity/range level
- HbA1c category

HbA1c is used for balancing and supportive analyses, not for Hard Gate determination.

### Blinding

The study is open-label with respect to treatment assignment and treatment duration. However, key endpoint events are adjudicated by an independent blinded CEC, and imaging evaluation uses centralized reading and adjudication processes where applicable. Exploratory biomarkers and prediction outputs are not available to frontline treatment teams by default.

### Data collection methods

Core data collected during the study include:

- Eligibility and baseline assessments
- Randomization and medication administration records
- Adherence and concomitant medication data
- Symptoms, physical findings, and tuberculosis-related event screening
- Smear, culture, and relevant molecular microbiology data
- Chest radiography and supplementary chest CT where clinically indicated
- Safety laboratories, ECGs, and QTc metrics
- Adverse event and serious adverse event reporting

### Retention and handling of missing data

The protocol includes multichannel contact, electronic reminders, graded tracing procedures, and passive outcome ascertainment where ethically permitted. If in-person visits cannot be completed, remote follow-up or locally obtained records may be used to reconstruct the minimum dataset required for primary endpoint determination.

### Data management

The trial uses EDC/IRT-based data capture, audit trails, role-based access control, and de-identification procedures. The data management framework includes standardized eCRF design, defined entry timelines, query management, prespecified edit checks, database cleaning, and lock procedures. Field-level derivation rules and technical implementation details are delegated to the DMP and related controlled documents.

### Safety reporting

AE, SAE, and AESI definitions follow the protocol and the dedicated safety management documents. Severity is graded using the DAIDS system. All SAEs are reported within protocol- and regulation-specified timelines. Particular attention is paid to hepatotoxicity, QTc prolongation and arrhythmia, ethambutol-related optic toxicity, severe hypersensitivity, and TB-DM comorbidity-related safety events such as severe hypoglycemia, DKA, and HHS.

### Monitoring

Monitoring and quality management focus on the processes and data most relevant to primary efficacy and safety conclusions, including:

- Eligibility and cohort allocation
- Randomization implementation
- Key microbiological and imaging assessments
- Completeness of endpoint event packages
- AE identification and reporting
- Follow-up completeness and loss-to-follow-up handling

Independent committees include a DSMB/DMC and a CEC. Risk-based quality management and central review procedures are supported by standardized SOPs.

## Statistical analysis

The trial uses a non-inferiority framework. In cohort A, the two primary comparisons are A1 versus A3 and A2 versus A3. In cohort B, the primary comparison is stopping treatment at 6 months versus continuing to 9 months among randomized participants who meet the Hard Gate.

For cohort A, the two primary comparisons use a Bonferroni-adjusted one-sided alpha of 0.0125. For cohort B, the one-sided alpha is 0.025, and the non-inferiority margin is 10%. The primary effect measure is the difference in relapse-free cure rates. The final definitions of analysis populations, missing-data handling, supportive analyses, and sensitivity analyses are specified in the SAP.

Participants in cohort B who do not meet the Hard Gate are not included in the main randomized treatment-effect comparison; their analyses are descriptive or exploratory. Missingness that prevents primary endpoint determination is handled conservatively in the main estimand and accompanied by prespecified sensitivity analyses.

A formal interim analysis is planned when approximately 50% of randomized participants have completed the primary endpoint assessment. O'Brien-Fleming-type alpha spending will be used, and the DSMB will review accumulating safety and operational data and make recommendations regarding continuation, modification, or suspension.

## Ethics and dissemination

The trial follows applicable GCP and ethics requirements. Written informed consent is required before any study-specific procedures. Privacy and confidentiality are protected through coded identifiers, restricted access, and controlled transfer and storage of research data.

Pregnant or breastfeeding women are not eligible. If pregnancy occurs during trial participation, study treatment is stopped and pregnancy exposure and maternal outcomes are followed according to protocol and regulatory requirements.

The results of the study are intended for dissemination through peer-reviewed publication and academic conference presentation.
