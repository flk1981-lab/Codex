## Abstract

### Background

Tuberculosis and diabetes mellitus frequently coexist and are associated with greater bacteriological burden, more complicated management, and higher recurrence risk after treatment. A single fixed treatment duration may therefore expose some patients with tuberculosis and diabetes mellitus to overtreatment and others to undertreatment. The TDSC study evaluates whether treatment duration can be shortened safely in selected lower-risk patients and whether treatment may be stopped at month 6 in selected higher-risk patients who satisfy a predefined microbiological stopping gate.

### Methods

TDSC is a multicenter, open-label, stratification-first randomized controlled trial in adults with drug-susceptible pulmonary tuberculosis and type 2 diabetes mellitus. At baseline, participants are assigned to a low-treatment-difficulty cohort or a high-treatment-difficulty cohort using a rule-based stratification tool centered on bacteriological burden and imaging severity. In cohort A, participants are randomized 1:1:1 to one of two 4-month rifapentine-containing regimens or to the 6-month standard regimen. In cohort B, all participants receive standard treatment to month 6; those who satisfy a predefined microbiological Hard Gate are randomized 1:1 to stop treatment at 6 months or continue HR to 9 months. The primary efficacy endpoint is relapse-free cure at 12 months after treatment cessation. Key endpoint events are adjudicated by an independent blinded clinical endpoint committee. All participants are followed for 24 months after treatment cessation.

### Discussion

This study is designed to generate operationally implementable evidence for individualized treatment-duration optimization in adults with tuberculosis and diabetes mellitus. The protocol prioritizes auditable clinical, microbiological, and imaging-based decision rules while reserving candidate biomarkers and prediction tools for prespecified validation and exploratory analyses.

## Introduction

### Background and rationale

Tuberculosis and diabetes mellitus increasingly overlap in high-burden settings, and diabetes mellitus is associated with higher mycobacterial burden, slower treatment response, more complex comorbidity management, and increased post-treatment recurrence risk. These features suggest that a uniform treatment duration may not be optimal for all adults with tuberculosis and diabetes mellitus.

Evidence supporting shortened regimens for drug-susceptible tuberculosis has expanded, particularly for rifapentine-containing regimens. However, currently available evidence does not directly answer whether shortened strategies can be applied safely and effectively to adults with tuberculosis and diabetes mellitus, nor does it adequately identify which higher-risk patients may safely stop treatment at 6 months rather than continue to 9 months.

The TDSC protocol therefore addresses two linked clinical questions within one stratification-first framework. First, among participants considered to have lower treatment difficulty at baseline, the study evaluates whether a fixed 4-month rifapentine-containing regimen is non-inferior to the standard 6-month regimen. Second, among participants considered to have higher treatment difficulty, the study evaluates whether a predefined month-6 microbiological Hard Gate can identify patients in whom treatment may safely stop at month 6. To improve reproducibility and multicenter scalability, treatment-changing decisions are anchored to standardized clinical, microbiological, and imaging-based rules, whereas candidate biomarkers and predictive models are reserved for prespecified validation and exploratory analyses.

### Objectives

#### Primary objectives

1. To determine, in cohort A, whether each of two 4-month rifapentine-containing regimens is non-inferior to the standard 6-month regimen with respect to relapse-free cure at 12 months after treatment cessation.
2. To determine, in cohort B, whether stopping treatment at 6 months in participants who satisfy the predefined Hard Gate is non-inferior to continuing treatment to 9 months with respect to relapse-free cure at 12 months after treatment cessation.

#### Secondary objectives

1. To compare relapse-free cure at 24 months after treatment cessation across treatment strategies.
2. To compare time to relapse, treatment failure, and reinfection-related outcomes.
3. To compare serious adverse events, grade 3 or higher adverse events, adverse events of special interest, and permanent treatment modifications due to adverse events.
4. To describe Hard Gate coverage, reasons for non-qualification, and downstream treatment pathways in cohort B.

#### Exploratory objectives

1. To evaluate the predictive performance and incremental value of candidate biomarkers, including TB22, CRISPR TB-DNA, and breathomics, for post-treatment recurrence.
2. To evaluate the supportive role of DSS, imaging AI, and related models in treatment-difficulty stratification.
3. To explore associations between pharmacokinetic exposure, glycemic control, and efficacy or safety outcomes.

### Trial design

TDSC is a multicenter, prospective, open-label, stratification-first randomized controlled trial. Participants are first allocated to one of two predefined cohorts according to treatment difficulty. Each cohort addresses a distinct but related treatment-optimization question within a common framework for screening, follow-up, endpoint adjudication, safety oversight, and data management.

In cohort A, participants with low treatment difficulty are randomized 1:1:1 to Arm A1, Arm A2, or Arm A3. In cohort B, participants with high treatment difficulty receive standard treatment to month 6. Those who meet the predefined microbiological Hard Gate are randomized 1:1 to stop treatment at month 6 or continue HR to month 9. Participants who do not meet the Hard Gate are not randomized but remain on protocol-specified treatment and follow-up pathways.

## Methods: participants, interventions, and outcomes

### Study setting

The trial is conducted at multiple centers with established capacity for tuberculosis diagnosis and management, diabetes care, microbiological testing, imaging evaluation, and clinical trial implementation. The coordinating center is Shenzhen Third People's Hospital.

### Eligibility criteria

#### Inclusion criteria

1. Written informed consent before any trial-specific procedures.
2. Age 18 to 70 years at consent.
3. Type 2 diabetes mellitus diagnosed according to accepted diagnostic criteria.
4. Active pulmonary tuberculosis supported by microbiological evidence, confirmation of rifamycin susceptibility, and compatible imaging findings; pulmonary disease may be accompanied by selected thoracic extrapulmonary involvement as permitted by the source protocol.
5. No prior anti-tuberculosis treatment for the current episode, or cumulative prior anti-tuberculosis treatment not exceeding 14 days.
6. Body weight between 30 kg and 90 kg at screening.
7. Negative pregnancy test in participants with reproductive potential and agreement to use effective contraception according to protocol requirements.
8. Investigator judgment that the participant is likely to complete treatment and long-term follow-up.

#### Exclusion criteria

1. Known or suspected resistance to core study drugs, or inability to exclude major resistance risk before randomization.
2. Severe extrapulmonary tuberculosis requiring specialized or prolonged treatment.
3. Strong evidence for non-tuberculous mycobacterial lung disease.
4. Acute metabolic decompensation related to diabetes requiring urgent inpatient management.
5. Severe chronic complications that may substantially interfere with safety assessment or adherence.
6. Clinically important hepatic, renal, hematologic, or electrolyte abnormalities beyond protocol thresholds.
7. Significant cardiac safety concerns, including major QTc prolongation or clinically important arrhythmia risk.
8. Required long-term use of drugs with major unavoidable interactions with rifamycins.
9. HIV infection, recent systemic corticosteroid exposure, active immunosuppressive or biologic therapy, or severe autoimmune disease as defined in the source protocol.
10. Other conditions judged by the investigator to materially affect safety, adherence, or endpoint assessment.
11. Pregnancy or breastfeeding.

### Who will obtain informed consent

Informed consent will be obtained by the investigator or authorized study staff who have received protocol- and site-specific training. The consent discussion will cover study purpose, treatment alternatives, expected follow-up, foreseeable risks and benefits, confidentiality protections, and the participant's right to withdraw.

### Interventions

#### Explanation for choice of comparators

The standard 6-month regimen serves as the active comparator because it represents established treatment for drug-susceptible pulmonary tuberculosis. The two rifapentine-containing regimens in cohort A were selected to test shorter treatment strategies that may retain acceptable efficacy while reducing treatment burden. In cohort B, continuation to 9 months after month-6 qualification is used as the comparator because it reflects the clinical question of whether treatment extension remains necessary in patients with initially higher treatment difficulty who show adequate microbiological response.

#### Intervention description

All study medications are administered orally once daily. Pyridoxine supplementation is used routinely. Weight-band dosing, formulation details, and dose-conversion rules are governed by controlled pharmacy documents.

- Cohort A, Arm A1: 2HP1200MZ followed by 2HP1200M
- Cohort A, Arm A2: 2HP900MZ followed by 2HP900M
- Cohort A, Arm A3: 2HRZE followed by 4HR
- Cohort B, months 0 to 2: H + R + Z + E
- Cohort B, months 2 to 6: H + R
- Cohort B month-6 decision point: Hard Gate assessment
- Cohort B if Hard Gate met: randomization to stop at month 6 or continue HR to month 9
- Cohort B if Hard Gate not met: no randomization; continuation of protocol-specified treatment and follow-up

#### Criteria for discontinuing or modifying allocated interventions

Allocated intervention may be interrupted, modified, or permanently discontinued for confirmed resistance, treatment failure, serious drug-related toxicity, clinically important QTc prolongation, optic toxicity suspected to be related to ethambutol, pregnancy, or any other event that makes continued protocol treatment unsafe. Participants who stop protocol treatment are retained in follow-up whenever possible so that endpoint ascertainment remains complete.

#### Strategies to improve adherence

Adherence support includes protocol training at treatment initiation, visit-based adherence review, medication accountability, contact reminders, and site-specific retention measures. Investigators document deviations, interruptions, and permanent changes to anti-tuberculosis treatment in the electronic data capture system.

#### Relevant concomitant care and interventions that are prohibited

All participants receive protocol-aligned management of tuberculosis-diabetes comorbidity, including glucose monitoring and optimization of anti-diabetic treatment. Concomitant drugs with major unavoidable rifamycin interactions, significant QT-prolonging liability, or substantial hepatotoxic risk should be avoided where possible. All concomitant medications, herbal products, and supplements are recorded.

### Outcomes

#### Primary outcome

The primary efficacy outcome is relapse-free cure at 12 months after treatment cessation. Key recurrence events are preferentially culture-confirmed and are evaluated with molecular typing or whole-genome sequencing where available to distinguish relapse from reinfection. Key endpoint events are adjudicated by a blinded clinical endpoint committee.

#### Key secondary outcomes

- Relapse-free cure at 24 months after treatment cessation
- Time to relapse
- Treatment failure
- Reinfection-related outcomes
- Serious adverse events, grade 3 or higher adverse events, and adverse events of special interest at prespecified time windows
- Permanent regimen modification due to inadequate efficacy or toxicity
- Hard Gate coverage and non-qualification patterns in cohort B

#### Safety outcomes

The main safety outcomes include serious adverse events, grade 3 or higher adverse events graded using the DAIDS system, and adverse events of special interest. Ethambutol-related optic toxicity, hepatotoxicity, clinically relevant QTc abnormalities, severe hypersensitivity reactions, and major diabetes-related metabolic events are monitored closely.

### Participant timeline

Key protocol timepoints include screening and baseline, weeks 2, 4, 8, and 12, week 16 for participants on 4-month regimens, month 6 or week 24 for the Hard Gate and end-of-treatment evaluation for 6-month regimens, week 36 for 9-month regimens, and post-treatment follow-up at 3, 6, 9, 12, 18, and 24 months after treatment cessation.

For manuscript preparation, a SPIRIT-style schedule summary is included in Appendix 2. If required by the submission system, a journal-formatted schedule figure can be inserted later without changing core protocol content.

### Sample size

The planned total sample size is 1350 participants, including approximately 850 in cohort A and 500 in cohort B. Cohort A is powered for two non-inferiority comparisons with multiplicity control. Cohort B assumes that approximately 50% to 60% of participants will satisfy the month-6 Hard Gate and undergo randomization, yielding an estimated randomized sample of approximately 250 to 300 participants.

### Recruitment

Participants will be recruited from tuberculosis clinics, inpatient wards, and routine tuberculosis care pathways at participating centers. Recruitment procedures will follow local regulatory and ethics requirements. Screening failures and reasons for non-enrolment will be documented according to site procedures and the protocol.

## Methods: assignment of interventions

### Sequence generation

Randomization sequences are generated centrally by independent statistical personnel using computerized permuted blocks. The exact block sizes are concealed from study sites.

### Allocation concealment mechanism

Allocation is implemented through the electronic randomization system. Treatment allocation is released only after completion of eligibility confirmation for the relevant randomization step.

### Implementation

Site investigators or authorized study staff enroll participants. Randomization is performed through the central system once eligibility is confirmed. In cohort A, randomization occurs at baseline. In cohort B, randomization occurs at month 6 only for participants who satisfy the Hard Gate.

### Blinding

The treatment assignment is open-label. To limit bias, key outcome events are adjudicated by an independent blinded clinical endpoint committee. Imaging review is centralized with independent assessment where required, and exploratory biomarkers are not routinely disclosed to treating teams.

## Methods: data collection, management, and analysis

### Data collection methods

Study data are collected from source documents, clinical assessments, microbiology testing, imaging evaluations, laboratory tests, electrocardiography, and participant follow-up records. Central outcome packages are prepared for adjudication of relapse, reinfection, treatment failure, death, and major efficacy-driven treatment changes.

### Retention and follow-up

Retention procedures include multi-channel contact information, scheduled reminders, and structured re-contact attempts for missed visits. Even when protocol treatment stops early, participants are encouraged to remain in follow-up so that primary and long-term outcomes can be assessed.

### Data management

Data are entered into an electronic data capture and randomization system with audit trails, role-based permissions, query resolution workflows, and data cleaning procedures. Dataset structure, derived variables, and database lock procedures are governed by the data management plan and statistical analysis plan.

### Statistical methods

The trial uses a non-inferiority framework. Cohort A evaluates A1 versus A3 and A2 versus A3 with multiplicity control. Cohort B evaluates the month-6 stopping strategy versus continuation to month 9 among randomized Hard Gate-qualified participants, with a non-inferiority margin of 10% according to the source protocol. Primary analyses, analysis populations, handling of missing data, sensitivity analyses, and secondary analyses will be prespecified in the statistical analysis plan.

### Interim analysis

One formal interim analysis is planned when approximately 50% of participants contributing to the primary efficacy analysis have completed the primary endpoint window. The interim review will use an O'Brien-Fleming-type alpha-spending approach. According to the source protocol, cohort A may stop further A1 enrolment after interim review if recommended by the data and safety monitoring board.

## Methods: monitoring

### Data monitoring

An independent data and safety monitoring board periodically reviews safety, recruitment, protocol adherence, and interim data according to its charter. The board provides recommendations on continuation, modification, or early termination of trial components.

### Harms

All adverse events, serious adverse events, and adverse events of special interest are collected, graded, and reported according to protocol-defined timelines. Suspected ethambutol-related optic toxicity triggers immediate discontinuation of ethambutol and urgent ophthalmologic evaluation.

### Auditing

The trial is subject to sponsor-led oversight, site monitoring, protocol training, and quality-control procedures. Additional audits may be conducted according to sponsor, institutional, or regulatory requirements, independently of the investigators where applicable.

## Discussion

The TDSC study seeks to answer a clinically important question that is under-addressed in the current tuberculosis treatment evidence base: how to optimize treatment duration in adults with tuberculosis and diabetes mellitus without sacrificing reproducibility or operational feasibility. The design deliberately separates treatment difficulty stratification from later treatment-stopping decisions and anchors the month-6 decision to auditable microbiological criteria rather than to complex or unstable composite judgments.

The protocol also balances pragmatism and methodological control. Open-label treatment is unavoidable, yet bias mitigation is strengthened through centralized randomization, blinded endpoint adjudication, centralized imaging review, and protocol-driven follow-up. If the trial is completed as planned, it may support a more individualized and scalable treatment framework for tuberculosis-diabetes comorbidity.
